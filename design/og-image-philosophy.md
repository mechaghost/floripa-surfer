# Faceted Horizon

A design philosophy.

## Manifesto

Communication through crystalline planes that catch and refract a single moment of light. The visible world is reduced to its essential geometry — triangles tessellate to suggest mountain, water, fire, atmosphere — and the meaning emerges not from realism but from the architecture of facets themselves. Each plane is a decision. Each edge is intentional. Nothing is rendered; everything is composed.

## Form and Space

The composition is governed by a single decisive horizon — the meeting of sea and sky — which bisects the field with classical certainty. Above: the slow vertical migration of sky color, banded but not blended. Below: a tessellated water surface, each facet a discrete prism of reflected sunset. A singular focal mass — the curl of a wave — disturbs the symmetry, drawing the eye into the lower-right field while the broader composition resolves around it. Hills recede in flat silhouette behind the central light source, layered with the restraint of woodblock printmaking. Negative space breathes at the periphery.

## Color and Material

The palette is meticulously restricted: marigold, terracotta, deep coral, ember magenta, petrol blue, midnight indigo, and the cream of sea foam. Warmth dominates the upper half — the language of a sun caught at the edge of the world — while cool depths anchor the lower field. Colors meet at hard edges. They do not blend. Each plane receives its own tone, and the harmony emerges from the painstaking calibration of relationships rather than the softness of transitions. This is the discipline of stained glass applied to landscape.

## Scale and Rhythm

A small dark form — a figure on the wave — provides the scalar key that gives the entire composition its monumentality. Without it, the field is abstract; with it, the wave becomes a cathedral. The rhythm of facets accelerates toward the focal point and slows at the edges, a careful orchestration of density that took countless hours to refine. Foam scatters as tiny crystalline marks across the wave's shoulder, each one placed by hand.

## Craftsmanship

This is not illustration. This is the work of a master engraver applied to a beach at dusk — the product of deep expertise, painstaking attention, master-level execution. Every triangle was considered for its position in both the chromatic and the spatial hierarchy. Every silhouette was redrawn until it held. The piece refuses photographic mimicry in favor of pure design intelligence, the kind of restraint that only emerges after years of study. It is meant to be looked at slowly, the way one looks at a print in a museum vitrine: surface first, then structure, then the quiet ache of the light.
